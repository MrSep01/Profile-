Final Clinical Practice Reflection - 12 Weeks of Growth in Teaching

APA-formatted reflection with in-text citations and references.

Final clinical practice reflection across twelve weeks of growth.

Introduction

Over the past 12 weeks of my final Clinical Practice as a chemistry teacher candidate at Moreland University, I have experienced profound growth in my teaching practice. This period has been a journey of applying theory to classroom reality, reflecting deeply on each lesson, and embracing feedback from mentors, instructors, and peers. In this reflection, I blend personal narrative with professional analysis to recount how I refined my teaching techniques through continuous improvement. I also align my development with the InTASC standards - a set of 10 core standards designed to promote effective teaching and growth (Council of Chief State School Officers [CCSSO], 2013). From enhancing student engagement and formative assessment strategies to integrating digital tools and project-based learning, I illustrate clear improvement in instructional effectiveness. I close with gratitude, an awareness of next steps, and a statement of my professional identity and commitment as an educator.

Growth Over 12 Weeks: Embracing Feedback and Reflection

Walking into my classroom 12 weeks ago, I felt both excited and uncertain. Today, I can see how much I have grown, thanks to a cycle of feedback and reflection. Early on, my mentor teacher observed my lessons twice a week and provided constructive "glows and grows." For example, in the first week she noted that while I explained chemistry content clearly (a glow), I needed to improve wait-time after asking questions (grow). I took this to heart and deliberately paused longer, which soon led to more student hands raised. Similarly, my university supervisor and course instructor gave feedback on lesson plans and observed teachings; their comments pushed me to refine my instructional planning and delivery. I recorded several classes and engaged in peer video reviews, where colleagues annotated my videos with feedback. This was eye-opening - a peer pointed out that I tended to call on the same few students. In response, I implemented a randomized name picker and small-group discussions, ensuring every learner's voice was heard. Through such iterations, I embraced continuous learning and improvement, welcoming every opportunity to evolve as an educator (Darling-Hammond et al., 2017). I strove to become the kind of reflective practitioner who continually evaluates the effects of my teaching on students and adapts my approach to meet each learner's needs (CCSSO, 2013). The evidence of growth was apparent: by week 12, my mentor remarked that my classroom had transformed into a more student-centered, engaging environment than it was at the start.

Video Lessons and Peer-Evaluated Feedback

One of the most impactful professional learning experiences was analyzing my teaching through video lessons. Mid-way through the practicum, I filmed a chemistry lesson on balancing chemical equations and shared it with peers for evaluation. Using an annotation tool, they left timestamped comments on everything from my pacing to student engagement levels. Initially, watching myself teach was daunting, but it led to powerful insights. I noticed, for instance, that I spent too much time lecturing and not enough time checking for student understanding. A peer's comment - "Consider inserting a quick formative check here" - prompted me to integrate a short poll in my next lesson. I also saw moments where student attention wavered, which aligned with my mentor's earlier feedback about varying my instructional strategies. Taking these observations, I refined my techniques: in subsequent lessons I broke content into smaller chunks, incorporated think-pair-share discussions after each chunk, and used visual aids to maintain interest. The improvement was clear in the next video review; peers noted that students appeared far more engaged and participatory. This cycle of plan - teach - video - peer review - adjust became a cornerstone of my growth. It not only honed specific skills (like clearer board work and better questioning techniques) but also instilled in me the habit of ongoing professional learning and evidence-based self-evaluation, exactly as InTASC Standard 9 envisions for effective teachers (CCSSO, 2013). By critically reviewing my own practice and acting on feedback, I became more intentional and reflective, qualities that I will carry forward into my teaching career.

Strategies for Student Engagement and Formative Assessment

Increasing student engagement and strengthening formative assessment were two priority areas in my teaching practice. Over the 12 weeks, I implemented several specific strategies to make my chemistry classes more interactive and to use assessment data to drive instruction. Key strategies included:

  • Project-Based Learning with Real-World Context: I designed authentic science inquiries, most notably the IonicFix water chemistry PBL unit, to tap into student curiosity. This PBL embodied sustained inquiry, real-world authenticity, student voice/choice, critique and revision, and a public product - all hallmarks of gold-standard PBL (PBLWorks, n.d.). For example, students investigated a local water quality issue and proposed solutions, which kept them highly motivated. Adopting a fun "Shark Tank" style pitch for project presentations further increased engagement while still demanding academic precision in chemistry reasoning and evidence-based planning (Hamari et al., 2014).
  • Gamified and Interactive Techniques: To grab attention and make learning fun, I infused lessons with interactive digital polls and challenges. The Shark Tank pitch mentioned above gamified the presentation experience, and I also used quick games like chemistry bingo and online quiz competitions. These age-appropriate formats boosted motivation without sacrificing rigor (Hamari et al., 2014), as students eagerly participated in order to "win" while still practicing key vocabulary and problem-solving.
  • Embedded Formative Assessments: I wove multiple formative checkpoints into every lesson to monitor understanding in real time. For instance, I frequently kicked off class with a Curipod word cloud or quick poll to gauge prior knowledge or moods. This provided instant class-wide data to surface misconceptions and adjust my lesson on the fly (Black & Wiliam, 1998). During lessons, I used "spot the mistake" diagnostic prompts and mid-lesson checks (like one-minute quiz questions or having students solve a problem on mini whiteboards) to ensure students were on track. Every lesson ended with a targeted exit ticket (often via Formative.com or Microsoft Forms) so I could quickly see who had mastered the day's objective and who needed reteaching (Black & Wiliam, 1998; Heritage, 2010). By balancing these objective-aligned formative assessments with occasional summative quizzes, I used data to drive my instruction daily.
  • Timely Feedback and Revision Opportunities: I established routines for giving timely, actionable feedback and creating time for students to use that feedback. One strategy was a peer review carousel: students rotated in groups to review each other's lab plans or reports using a Glow/Grow feedback checklist, leaving at least one "Glow" (strength) and one "Grow" (suggestion) for improvement. This structured peer critique not only engaged students as collaborators but also trained them to think critically about quality work. It produced documented feedback for each group and was followed by requiring each team to submit a brief "Revision Plan" of the changes they would make based on the feedback. I also gave quick micro-conferences - e.g., a one-minute chat with each group during work time - to provide one targeted next step, which I'd record in their Teams chat for reference. Because students knew they would immediately revise their work, they took the feedback more seriously. This feedback loop echoes widely used assessment-for-learning principles emphasizing timely feedback and instructional responsiveness (Hattie & Timperley, 2007; Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick, 2006). It was rewarding to see students improve their work within the same lesson or by the next day, a clear indication that formative assessment was improving their learning.
  • Differentiation and Inclusive Practices: To keep every student engaged, I differentiated my instruction based on formative data. For example, during the IonicFix project, after reviewing exit ticket responses I grouped students by need: those who were secure moved on to an extension activity, while those needing support got a mini-lesson re-teach the next day. I prepared "ion menus" and scaffolded guides for groups that were stuck during the project, and provided challenge problems for advanced learners. I also varied modalities - incorporating visuals, hands-on experiments, discussions, and written reflections - so that learners with different strengths could all access the content. These approaches created a more inclusive environment (addressing InTASC Standards 1 and 2 on learner development and differences) and kept students of all backgrounds engaged. I learned to continuously monitor and adjust supports mid-lesson, for instance by offering alternative methods when I saw several groups struggling (Tomlinson, 2017; CAST, 2018). By embedding multiple ways to demonstrate learning (written, oral, visual) and by encouraging student self-assessment against criteria, I enabled each student to find success (CAST, 2018). My classroom evolved into a place where it was normal to see different groups doing different tasks - each actively learning at their own pace and level.

Implementing these strategies dramatically increased student engagement and improved my science instruction. Classroom observations showed nearly all students actively participating, whether through answering polls, contributing ideas in group work, or giving feedback to peers. Moreover, the constant formative feedback meant I was no longer "teaching blind" - instead, I had a clear, data-informed picture each day of who was grasping the material and who wasn't (Black & Wiliam, 1998). This allowed me to be a more responsive teacher, adapting my plans to student needs rather than rigidly sticking to a script. The result was more effective instruction and higher student achievement on end-of-unit assessments, as confirmed by my mentor's tracking of scores. In short, engaging students through interactive, student-centered techniques and using formative assessment as a compass has made me a far more effective science teacher than I was 12 weeks ago.

Integrating Digital Tools and Inquiry-Based Learning

A significant aspect of my growth was learning to integrate digital tools with inquiry-based, student-centered learning. Coming into this practicum, I was moderately comfortable with technology but unsure how to leverage it meaningfully for learning. Through experimentation and advice from peers, I discovered the power of tools like Curipod and Formative.com in enhancing inquiry lessons. Curipod became a go-to for me - I used its features to create live word clouds and quizzes that made classroom brainstorming and diagnostics exciting. For example, when starting the IonicFix water chemistry unit, I had students respond via Curipod to "In one word, how do you feel about science today?" which instantly created a word cloud of their feelings (a mix of "curious," "confused," "excited"). Not only did this serve as a fun icebreaker, it also gave me a quick read on the class's mindset (and even their confidence levels) (Black & Wiliam, 1998). Curipod polls also helped us discuss real-world context - I polled "Who is affected by water pollution in our community?" and "What constraint matters most (cost, safety, time)?" to kick off the project, which got students thinking critically and sharing ideas right away (PBLWorks, n.d.). Meanwhile, Formative (goformative.com) was invaluable for more structured formative assessments. I created online quizzes and assignments where students could submit answers that I viewed in real time. During a lesson on ionic bonding, I put a few practice problems on Formative; as students submitted answers on their devices, I could see on my screen who was getting it and who was struggling. This immediate insight let me address misconceptions on the spot (for instance, re-teaching how to balance charges in ionic compounds when I saw 40% of the class missed that question). Students later told me they liked the instant feedback they got from Formative's auto-grading on some questions, and the fact that I would often respond in-app with quick comments or hints for improvement.

Beyond tech tools, I also embraced inquiry-based learning strategies such as project-based learning (PBL) and lab investigations. The IonicFix unit was the centerpiece of this effort (detailed below), but even in regular lessons I shifted toward more inquiry. Instead of simply demonstrating chemistry phenomena, I began to pose questions and challenges for students to explore. For instance, rather than explaining solubility rules outright, I gave small groups different salts, water, and a conductivity tester and asked them to investigate which solutions conduct electricity and why. This guided inquiry led students to discover the concept of ions carrying charge in solution. I found that when students had a personal hand in uncovering scientific principles, they understood and retained the content better. My instructor noted in one observation that I was "facilitating learning rather than just dispensing knowledge" - a comment that signified a big step forward for me in aligning with InTASC Standard 5 (connecting concepts to engage learners in problem-solving) and Standard 8 (using varied instructional strategies for deep understanding) (CCSSO, 2013).

Crucially, I learned to integrate technology in service of inquiry, not as a distraction or gimmick. Each digital tool had a purpose: Curipod for capturing student thinking and misconceptions visually, Formative for real-time assessment data, and even Microsoft Teams for collaboration (students in my classes used Teams to share documents and I used it to distribute resources and give feedback). By combining these tools with hands-on, minds-on inquiry activities, I created a more modern and engaging science classroom. Students were not just consuming information; they were using digital platforms to create, collaborate, and investigate. One student even commented that our classroom felt "like a science lab and a tech startup at the same time," which I took as a compliment to the dynamic learning environment. Moving forward, I am excited to keep exploring new technologies (like simulations, virtual labs, or data analysis tools) to further enrich inquiry-based science instruction.

Project Highlight: The IonicFix PBL Unit (Water Chemistry)

One of the most transformative experiences of my clinical practice was the design and implementation of a student-centered PBL unit called "IonicFix." This project-based learning unit was a culmination of everything I had been learning about engaging instruction, assessment integration, and real-world relevance. The premise of IonicFix was that students, as young scientists, were tasked with improving a local water quality issue in Thailand. We framed it as: a mysterious water symptom (for example, a village stream turning milky-white and bitter, with dead shrimp observed) needs investigating and fixing. Students worked in teams to identify possible ionic compounds causing the issue and then to propose a feasible solution to improve the water. The overarching unit goal was for students to apply core chemistry concepts (ions, bonding, stoichiometry, etc.) to design and justify a solution to a community water problem and communicate it through a pitch and prototype (CCSSO, 2013; PBLWorks, n.d.). This authentic problem gave learning a real sense of purpose. As one student said during the project, "I feel like a scientist solving something that actually matters in the world." Hearing that was incredibly rewarding.

Planning and Alignment: In designing the unit, I used the Moreland PBL lesson template and kept the InTASC standards 6-8 evidence expectations in mind, to ensure my plans included solid assessment, planning, and strategy use (CCSSO, 2013). I also incorporated established best practices in PBL, drawing from the literature. Specifically, I ensured there was sustained inquiry, authenticity, student voice and choice, critique and revision, and a public product (PBLWorks, n.d.). These elements were built into IonicFix: students chose which local water issue to tackle (voice/choice), the problems were real and from their own communities (authenticity), the project spanned multiple weeks of research and experimentation (sustained inquiry), we embedded peer critique and iterative improvement (critique & revision), and it culminated in a final presentation to an audience (public product). I even adopted a "Shark Tank" pitch format for the final presentations, where each team had to pitch their water solution in a 5-minute video to a panel (our panel included a science teacher, a school administrator, and a parent volunteer acting as community members). This Shark Tank approach was chosen as an age-appropriate format to boost motivation while still holding students to high academic expectations in terms of scientific reasoning and evidence (Hamari et al., 2014). Indeed, it energized the class - students were excited by the friendly competition and the idea of "selling" their solution, yet they knew they had to have solid data and a workable prototype to convince the judges.

Execution and Learning: Implementing the IonicFix project taught me a great deal about facilitating student-centered learning. In the first phase, students conducted a needs analysis of their chosen water issue, articulating the problem in terms of a clear problem statement: location + symptom + suspected ion/compound + a measurable way to test it (National Research Council, 2012). I guided them with a "spot the mistake" whole-class activity to practice writing testable problem statements, then gave each group a framework to draft their own. Using a micro-rubric, I quickly reviewed each group's problem statement and marked it "Ready" or "Revise," giving a one-sentence suggestion for improvement in Teams. This immediate feedback loop ensured that groups had a solid foundation before moving on. Next, students had to justify why their chosen ion was likely causing the issue, using at least one credible source. I held mini-conferences with each group, asking, "What evidence do you have for that ion?" and nudging them to find or cite a source. Here I acted more as a coach, pointing them to resources or giving hints rather than just telling them answers.

The heart of the project was the investigation plan. Each team drafted a one-page proposal outlining their hypothesis, variables (IV, DV, controls), materials, procedure, data table design, and success criteria for "improved water" (National Research Council, 2012). Crafting this plan was challenging for many, so I broke it into parts: one lesson focused on variables and hypothesis, another on method and safety, etc. I used formative checkpoints at each step - for example, after a mini-lesson on identifying independent vs. dependent variables, I had teams fill out just the IV/DV section of their plan and submit it. I then quickly checked each for correctness using a short checklist (IV measurable? DV measurable? controls identified?) and immediately provided help: groups that misunderstood variables got a quick re-teach, while advanced groups were prompted to consider reliability (trials) or an error reduction strategy (Black & Wiliam, 1998; Tomlinson, 2017). This just-in-time support kept all teams moving forward without frustration. It was amazing to see students iterate on their scientific methods in real-time; they would get feedback at 10am and by 10:15am be back at their desks revising their plans.

Peer Review and Revision Culture: One of the highlights of IonicFix was building a classroom culture of critique and revision. After drafting their investigation plans and pitch outlines, teams participated in a peer review carousel. Using a structured checklist that asked questions like "Is the problem statement clear and testable?" and "Does the solution address the need?" each team reviewed another team's plan. They had to give at least one Glow (praise) and one Grow (suggestion). Students took this seriously - I saw them huddled around another team's poster, debating what feedback to write. After the carousel, every team had peer feedback to consider. Crucially, we didn't stop there: I required a "Revision Plan" from each group, where they had to list two specific changes they would make based on the feedback. This step was vital in teaching students to actually use feedback to improve, not just receive it. It reinforced the idea that first drafts aren't final and that even their project was a learning process. The result was improved quality in their final proposals - for example, one group's initial test plan was vague about measurements, but after peer critique they added detailed steps with specific volumes and times, making their experiment much more solid. The peer review process not only improved the projects but also empowered students to act as experts and collaborators in each other's learning (Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick, 2006).

Outcomes and Reflections: In the final stage, teams created their pitch decks and prototypes. Many built simple physical prototypes (like a mini water filter using charcoal and sand, or a chemical treatment kit demonstration) to accompany their presentations. The Shark Tank pitch presentations were a resounding success - students spoke with confidence about chemistry concepts like precipitate reactions or ion exchange as they justified their solutions. The panel was impressed by the creativity and scientific rigor the students showed. Perhaps more importantly, the students themselves were proud. One quiet student, who rarely spoke up in regular lessons, absolutely shone during her team's presentation, articulating the science behind their prototype passionately. It was a powerful reminder of how giving students ownership can draw out their potential.

For me as a teacher candidate, IonicFix was a milestone. It tested my ability to plan a complex unit, manage a noisy collaborative classroom, differentiate support for each group, and trust my students as capable scientists. It wasn't always smooth - there were moments of controlled chaos, and I had to flexibly adjust timelines. But the experience improved my confidence tremendously. It demonstrated growth in InTASC Standard 7 (Planning for Instruction), since I had to carefully design a sequence of learning activities and scaffolds; in Standard 6 (Assessment), as I embedded varied formative assessments and adjusted teaching based on them (CCSSO, 2013); and in Standard 5 (Application of Content), as I facilitated learners in solving an authentic local problem using chemistry (CCSSO, 2013; PBLWorks, n.d.). Perhaps most significantly, I saw firsthand how aligning my teaching with research-based best practices leads to better student outcomes. The PBLWorks "gold standard" elements I had read about were not just buzzwords - when put into practice, sustained inquiry, student voice, and a culture of critique truly ignited my students' engagement and learning (PBLWorks, n.d.). This project affirmed my belief that science education should be student-centered and relevant to the real world. It's an approach I am committed to carrying forward as part of my professional identity.

The Value of Feedback Cycles and Professional Development

Throughout my clinical practice, a recurring theme has been the value of feedback cycles for professional growth. In addition to informal feedback from teaching videos and mentor observations, the program's formal assignments created structured feedback opportunities that greatly enhanced my development. For instance, early in the semester I completed a Professional Growth Plan assignment where I outlined goals (mine focused on improving differentiation and formative assessment techniques). My instructor provided detailed feedback on that plan, challenging me to make my goals more specific and measurable. I revised the plan and then actively worked on those goals each week, effectively using it as a roadmap. Periodically, I revisited my progress: did I try the new questioning technique I promised to? Was I seeing improvement in student responses as evidence? This reflective goal-tracking ensured that the feedback from that initial assignment wasn't lost; instead, it became a living part of my practice.

Similarly, our cohort engaged in professional discussions and roadmap check-ins. In these sessions, we shared challenges and success strategies. Hearing a peer describe how he increased student talk time in his classroom gave me new ideas (I later implemented a "student question time" inspired by him, where I let students lead part of the review by asking their own questions). My participation in these cohort discussions and external webinars (such as a science teaching webinar on phenomena-based learning) helped me begin building a professional learning network, connecting me with educators beyond my school. This aligns with the program's expectations for outstanding teacher candidates to engage in meaningful professional development beyond just our coursework and to start forming a network in the education community (Darling-Hammond et al., 2017). I can proudly say I've done that - I joined an online group for chemistry teachers where we exchange lesson ideas, and I attended a local science teacher meet-up which led to exchanging contact info with a couple of experienced teachers who offered to mentor me informally.

Another key feedback cycle was the formal evaluation process by my university supervisor. After each formal observation, we had a debrief meeting. In these meetings, we would go over the InTASC standards and my evidence for each. At first, this felt a bit like a checklist exercise, but I soon realized how valuable it was in pinpointing areas of improvement. For example, after an observation in week 5, my supervisor noted that while I met Standard 4 (Content Knowledge) well, I needed to put more focus on Standard 2 (Learning Differences) - specifically, to better incorporate students' cultural backgrounds into lessons. We brainstormed how I could do this, and as a result I made a change in my teaching: I began to invite students to share examples or applications from their home or culture. During our unit on pH, one student talked about how acid rain affected the village her grandparents live in - this opened up a rich discussion and validated her experience. By the final observation, I had intentionally designed culturally relevant hooks or examples in each unit, and my supervisor commended my growth in creating inclusive, culturally responsive learning experiences. This example underscores how being receptive to feedback and willing to change resulted in concrete improvements in my teaching practice. It's exactly the mindset described in InTASC Standard 9 - embracing ongoing learning and examining the impact of my choices on learners (CCSSO, 2013).

Finally, as part of our culmination, I developed a Professional Development Plan for my first year of teaching. In it, I identified future growth opportunities and set goals to continue strengthening my instruction based on student needs (for instance, I want to improve at accommodating special needs students in labs, so I plan to attend a workshop on inclusive science labs). Crafting this plan was a reflective experience that made me appreciate the continuous nature of teacher development. It also reinforced that I am now part of a larger professional community - I sought input from my mentor on which local PD events to attend, and I included joining the National Science Teachers Association (NSTA) as a goal to widen my network. In essence, the cycle of feedback doesn't end with this clinical practice; I am carrying it forward. I acknowledge that continuous learning and improvement can positively impact my teaching and ultimately student success (Darling-Hammond et al., 2017). The process of feedback - whether from mentors, peers, students, or self-reflection - will remain a staple of how I grow and refine my practice throughout my career.

Leadership and the Learning Commons Initiative

An unexpected area of growth for me during this practicum was stepping into a leadership role at my placement school. Midway through the semester, I was invited by my cooperating mentor and the school's academic coordinator to help develop a new "Parent-Teacher-Student Learning Commons" initiative. The idea was to transform our school's traditional library into a modern learning commons - a flexible, collaborative space that would serve as a hub for students, teachers, and parents to engage in learning together. This initiative was truly a whole-school project, and being part of the design team was both challenging and enlightening.

My role initially involved research and gathering input. I surveyed students about what would make the library more inviting (their answers: comfy seating, a makerspace corner, and longer hours after school). I also attended a PTA meeting with my mentor to hear parent perspectives - parents were eager for a space where they could come after school to read with their kids or attend mini-workshops on supporting learning at home. Using this input, our team (which included two other teachers and a librarian) drafted a proposal for the Learning Commons. I contributed by sketching a floorplan that included zones for quiet reading, collaborative work, and a small "innovation lab" area with science kits and computers. We envisioned the Learning Commons as "the physical and virtual collaborative learning hub of the school" that emphasizes creativity, collaboration, and opportunities to enhance learning outcomes (American Association of School Librarians [AASL], 2018). In practical terms, we proposed extended hours one day a week where parents could come with students to work on homework or projects with teacher support available - a sort of community study night. We also planned rotating monthly events, like a Family STEM Night, a parent-child book club meeting, and student project exhibitions in the space.

Helping design this initiative allowed me to exercise leadership and collaboration skills beyond my individual classroom - very much in line with InTASC Standard 10, which encourages teachers to seek leadership roles and collaborate with families and the community to ensure learner growth (CCSSO, 2013). I experienced first-hand what it's like to work with various stakeholders towards a common educational vision. It pushed me to consider perspectives outside the classroom: How can we make the space welcoming for parents who might not speak much English? What resources do teachers need to effectively use the space for cross-curricular projects? How do we align the Learning Commons activities with school improvement goals? I found myself co-leading a small presentation to the school leadership team to advocate for the plan, which was a great professional experience in communication and persuasion. Ultimately, the administration approved a pilot of the Learning Commons concept for next semester.

Through this project, I gained confidence in my ability to contribute to school-wide improvement efforts. I learned that being a teacher-leader is not about title or position, but about initiative and collaboration. Even as a student teacher, I was able to offer meaningful input and rally others around an idea that benefits students. This has shaped my professional identity - I see myself not just as a teacher confined to my classroom, but as an educator who is part of a broader community, willing to step up and lead when there's an opportunity to improve student learning beyond the four walls of my room. It was also heartening to see the excitement from students when we discussed the upcoming Learning Commons; several came to me with suggestions (one even drew her own idea of how the space should look). Involving students in the process was a reminder that leadership in education often means empowering student voice as well. All in all, the Learning Commons initiative became a highlight of my clinical practice, illustrating that my growth wasn't only in teaching techniques but also in collaborative leadership. I carry forward a strong commitment to continue taking on leadership roles in my future school - whether it's leading a team meeting, mentoring a colleague, or spearheading a new project - in order to advance the profession and create the best possible learning environments for students (CCSSO, 2013).

Conclusion: Gratitude, Next Steps, and Professional Identity

As I conclude this final reflection, I am filled with gratitude. I am deeply thankful to my mentor teacher, whose steady guidance, candid feedback, and constant encouragement created the perfect environment for me to learn and take risks. I am grateful to my university instructors and supervisors, who challenged me to align my practice with high standards and who were always available with advice or a probing question that made me think deeper. I also owe thanks to my peers in the cohort - our collaborative spirit (through peer reviews, discussion boards, and coffee chats swapping ideas) proved that we truly are better together in this journey. And of course, I am grateful to my students. They were the center of it all - every strategy I tried, every tool I used, was for them. Their enthusiasm, honesty, occasional frustration, and eventual successes taught me more about teaching (and myself) than any textbook could.

Looking ahead, I feel a strong sense of purpose and clarity about my next steps. I plan to carry forward the momentum of growth from these 12 weeks straight into my first year of full-time teaching. Concretely, my next steps include executing the Professional Development Plan I created: joining the NSTA, attending a summer workshop on PBL in science, and continuing to improve my practice in differentiation and assessment as outlined in my goals. I will also actively seek out a mentor in my new school and maintain ties with the professional learning network I've begun to build, because I know now that teaching is not a solitary endeavor - it flourishes with collaboration and support. Furthermore, I intend to keep reflecting regularly (through journaling or maybe even blogging about my experiences) to ensure I remain a reflective practitioner. In fact, this very reflection is something I could adapt into a blog post to share with others, as part of my commitment to ongoing learning and sharing in the teaching community.

In terms of my professional identity, I now confidently see myself as a chemistry educator and lifelong learner. I am a teacher who believes in the power of student-centered learning - whether it's through an engaging PBL unit like IonicFix or simply a well-timed question that sparks a great class discussion. I am committed to using formative assessment and feedback to guide students towards growth, echoing my core belief that every student can learn and improve when given the right support and high expectations. I also identify as a teacher-leader in the making, one who is eager to collaborate with colleagues and contribute positively to the school community. Most importantly, I am an educator who puts students first: their curiosity, their needs, their cultures, their voices. This experience has reinforced for me that teaching is far more than delivering content - it is about inspiring students, continually improving my craft, and building a community of learners that extends beyond the classroom.

With humility and excitement, I conclude this chapter of my journey. I know there is so much more to learn - and I am ready. I will step into my next classroom carrying the lessons, experiences, and confidence these 12 weeks have given me. Thank you to everyone who has been part of this transformative journey. I move forward with gratitude, a commitment to excellence and equity in education, and the unwavering knowledge that teaching is my calling. I am proud to now join the ranks of professional educators, and I am committed to making a positive difference in the lives of my students, every day, as a reflective, innovative, and compassionate chemistry teacher. Here's to the journey ahead - one of continual growth, collaboration, and impactful teaching.
